Introduction

Introduction

The screen that swallowed our days.

You are not just holding your phone. Your phone is holding you. You open it to answer one message, then somehow you come back half an hour later after watching ten people's lives, getting angry at a man who does not even know you exist, and laughing at a clip you will forget in a few minutes. Then you turn the screen off, but part of you stays there: your time, your calm, and maybe even your heart.

We are not really living. A lot of the time, we are just scrolling. In the morning, your hand reaches for the phone before your heart even gets its peace back. You enter everyone else's mind before you enter your own day. You know what they did, what they bought, and where they went, but you still do not know what you should do with your first hour. The problem is not just a small device. It is an open door that lets the noise of the world walk straight into you all day.

The phone has become a kind of numbness. When you are bored, you open it. When you feel tight inside, you open it. When you are scared of a task, you open it. It became one answer for every question. But it does not fix the problem. It only delays the feeling. It calms the anxiety for a moment, then sends it back heavier, because the task is still there, the prayer you delayed is still on you, and the decision you are running from has not changed.

The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said that many people are cheated when it comes to two blessings: health and free time. Being cheated means owning something valuable, then selling it for almost nothing. We give the clearness of the morning to notifications and the quiet of the night to videos, then we say, "I do not have time." The truth is, the time is there. It is just leaking through tiny holes we do not notice.

You are not only watching. You are being shaped. What you see again and again starts to feel lighter in your heart. What you keep hearing becomes part of your language. What you keep comparing yourself to changes your contentment. So do not say, "I am just watching." There is no repeated looking without an effect. The screen may turn off, but the images stay inside.

Do not let this introduction be another reminder you scroll past. Start with one thing: do not give your phone the first hour of your day. Get up, remember Allah, pray, drink water, and write down the most important thing you need to do. Before every time you open the screen, ask yourself, "Why am I opening this?" If you do not have a clear answer, do not open it.

In the next chapter, we will get closer to the thief living in your pocket, and we will see how tiny minutes turn into lost years while you keep smiling and saying, "It is just a few minutes."

This book is from the Wathaker series. It is written for the young Muslim who wants to take back his heart, his mind, and his time from a screen that never gets full.

This book is educational and awareness-based. It does not replace professional help if someone is dealing with a serious mental or behavioral disorder.

The quoted texts in this book rely on specific sources: Sahih al-Bukhari, Siyar A'lam al-Nubala by al-Dhahabi, Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, and Bahjat al-Majalis by Ibn Abd al-Barr.
